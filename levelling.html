Levelling

It is the branch of surveying which is used to find the elevation from the datum line.

The art of the determing the relative heights of different points on or blow the surface of the earth is known as levelling.

Object and Purpose of Levelling:

The Objects of the levelling is:

  1. To find the elevation of given points with respect to a given or assumed datum,and.
  2. To Establish points at a given elevation or at different elevations with respect to a given or assumed datum.

Levelling is Performed for the Following Purposes :

  • To prepare a contour map used for various engineering works.
    • Examples:
    1. To fix the site for Reservoirs,Dams,Barrages etc.
    2. To fix the alignment of Roads,Railways,Irrigation canals etc.
  • To prepare a longitudinal section and cross-sections of Roads, Railways,Irrigation canals etc.,in order to calculate the volume of earth work.
  • To prepare a layout map for water supply,sanitary or drainage schemes.
  • To fix bench marks at prominent places.
  • To set the depths of excavation in foundations.
  • To transfer the bench mark to the work site.

Definition Of Terms Used in Levelling :

  1. Level Surface :

    2. Any surface parallel to the mean spheridal surface of the earth,is said to be a level surface.

    Example:The water surface of a still lake is considered to be a level surface.

  2. Level Line :

    3. Any line lying on a level surface is called a level line.

  3. Horizontal Plane :

    4. A plane tangential to the level surface at any point,is known as a horizontal plane.It is perpendicular to the plumbline at the tangent point.

  4. Horizontal Line :

    5. A line lying in the horizontal plane,is also known as a horizontal line. It is a straight line tangential to the level line.

  5. Vertical Line :

    6. Vertical line at a point is the direction of gravity(i.e.,towards the centre of the earth), as indicated by a plumb line.This line is perpendicular to the horizontal line.

  6. Vertical Angle :

    7. The angle between an inclined line and a horizontal line at a plane,in vertical plane,is called vertical angle.

  7. Datum Surface:

    8. The imaginary level surface with reference to which vertical distances of differnt points are measured,is called Datum Surface.

  8. Mean Sea Level(MSL):

    9. This is the mean level of the sea obtained by taking the average of all tide heights as measured at hourly interval over some specified period.

  9. Elevation or Reduced Level(RL):

    10. The vertical distance of a point above or below the datum line is known as the Elevation or Reduced Level(RL) of that point.

  10. Line of Collination :

    11. It is an imaginary line joining the intersection of the cross-hairs at the diaph ragm to the optical centre of the objective and its continuation.It is also called Line of sight.

  11. Axis of the Telescope:

    12. It is the line joining the optical centre of the objective to the optical of the eye-piece.

  12. Axis of Level Tube or Bubble Tube:

    13. It is an imaginary line tangential to the longitudinal curve of the bubble tube and its middle point .

  13. Back Sight(BS):

    14. This is the first staff reading taken in any setup of the instrument after the levelling has been perfectly done.It is also called as Plus sight.

  14. Fore Sight(FS):

    15. It is last staff reading taken from any setup of the instrument,on a point whose elevation is to be determined.

    Example : a sight on a change point,i.e., station B. This is also called as Minus sight.

  15. Intermediate Sight(IS):

    16. It is any staff reading taken on a point of unknown elevation between backsight and foresight.

    Example : a sight on station C

  16. Change Point(C.P.) Or Turning Point(T.P.):

    17. It is a point denoting the shifting of the level.,on this point,an FS is taken from one setting and a BS from the next setting.

    Example:Station 'B'

  17. Height of Instrument(HI):

    18. When the levelling instrument is properly levelled,the RL of the line of collision is known as the Height of the Instrument.

  18. Station:

    19. A point,whose elevation is to be determined is called station.The staff is kept on this points.

    Example :A,B,C etc.

  19. Parallax:

    20. The apparent movement of the image relative to the cross-hairs is known as parallax.

  20. Bench - Mark(BM):

    21. It is a fixed reference point of known Elevation.

    Depending on the permanency and precisim,bench marks may be of the the following types :

    1. Great Trigonometric Survey(GTS) Bench Marks(BM) :

      2. These are established by the Survey of India Department at an interval of about 100km all over the country,with respect to the mean sea level at karachi as datum.

    2. Permanent Bench Marks:

      3. These are established with respect to G.T.S.Bench marks by different Government. Department like P.W.D,Railways,Irrigation,etc.on permanent points like the plinth of the building.

    3. Arbitrary Bench Marks:

      4. These are reference points whose elevations are arbitrarily assumed in small levelling operations.

    4. Temporary Bench Marks:

      5. The bench-marks established temporarily at the end of a day's work and from where levelling is continued in the next day,are called Temporary Bench-Marks.

Types of Levelling Instruments:

The following are the different types of levels:

  1. Dumpy Level:

    2. A levelling instrument,also called the level,is used for the determination levels.The dumpy level is commodity used for levelling is designed by Gravity.

    Description of Dumpy level:
    1. Tripod stand:

      2. The level is fitted to the tripod stand while taking the levels in the field.It consists of a head and three legs with pointed metal shoes.

      1. Tripod with constant Length Legs:

        2. The fixed-leg tripods provide more rigidity and stability to the levelling instrument,and are,therefore,preferred.

      2. Tripod with Extensive Legs:

        3. This type of tripod provide more flexibility,as the legs can be adjusted to suit any suitable length.

    2. Levelling Head:

      3. The Levelling head consists of two parallel plates held apart by three(or four) levelling screws The upper plate is called tribrach.

    3. Foot Screws(or Levelling Screws):

      4. Three(or four)foot screws are provided between the trivet and tribach.By turning the foot screws the tribach can be raised or lowered to bring the bubble to the centre of its run.

    4. Telescope:

      5. The Telescope is fixed on a vertical spindle.The telescope tube and the vertical spindle are cast as one piece.The spindle revolves in the socket of the levelling head. It also consists of an object glass and an eye-piece on opposite ends.A diaphragm is fixed with in the telescope just infront of the eye-piece.The diaphragm carries cross-hairs.The telescope is focussed by means of a focussing screw.

      Depending upon the arrangement provided for bringing the image of the object formed by the objective exactly in the plane of cross-hairs,there are two types of telescopes.

      1. External-focussing telescope.
      2. Internal-focussing telescope.

      1. External Focussing Telescope:

        2. In the external focussing telescope,the objective and the eye-piece are mounted on two different tubes so that one can slide inside the others.

      2. Internal Focussing Telescope:

        3. In the internal focussing telescope,the objective and the eye-piece are in fixed position.

    5. Bubble tubes:

      6. Two bubbles tubes,one is called longitudinal bubble tube and other the cross-bubble tube,are placed at right angles to each other on the top of the telescope or on its one side.These tubes contain spirit bubble.The bubble is brought to the centre with help of the foot screw.

    6. Diaphragm:

      7. The surveying telescopes are designed in such a manner,that the image of t he object is always formed in a fixed plane,when the instrument has been focussed. The fixed plane at which the image is formed is always made to lie in the plane at which the images is formed is always made to lie in the plane of the cross-hairs in a ring called is 'diaphragm'.

      Diaphragm

      The general arrangement of cross-hairs on a diaphragm.The middle horizontal hairs representing the line of collimation.The two vertical hairs are meant for maintaining the verticality of the staff.

      It also consists of a two additional horizontal hairs,one above and the others below the central horizontal hairs.These hairs are called stadia hairs,and are used in stadia tachemetry for computing the horizontal and vertical distances from the instrument to that of the staff.

    Compass

    A compass is provided below the telescope used for taking the magnetic bearing of a line when required.

    Levelling Staff :

    A Levelling Staff is a straight rectangular,wooden or Aluminium and graduated into meters and smaller divisions.The levelling staves of 3m and 4m lengths are commonly used in practice.

    Each metre is divided into 200 divisions.The thickness of each division is 5 mm or 0.005m.The divisions and painted alternatively black and white.

Types of Levelling Stoves :

Based on the method of taking reading,the levelling stave are divided into two types :

  1. Self-reading staff
  2. Target staff

A target staff contains a moving target attached to the staff,which can be slided up or down by the staff man,as per the instructions given by the instrument man,till the line of sight bisects the target.

Based on the method of Construction,the levelling staves are divided into three types:

  1. Solid staff
  2. Folding staff
  3. Telescopic staff(Sop with pattern).
  1. Solid Staff

    2. The solid staff is in one length of 3m.As there is no joints or hinges in between, there is no possibility of relative movement of differnt sections and the staff readings are quite accurate.Hence,this staff is used for precise levelling work.

  2. Folding Staff

    3. The Staff is made of well-seasoned timber,and is of width 75mm,thickness 18mm, and length a locking arrangement.

  3. Telescopic Staff

    4. A telescopic staff consists of a three pieces,which can be extended to a full length of 4m.

Temporary Adjustments of a Dumpy level

The following temporary adjustments are done in the case of a dumpy level.

  1. Setting Up:

    2. As the level is not to be set at any fixed point,the setting up of level is much simpler than compared to other instruments.However,while fixing the position of a levelling instrument.The following points can be remembered.

    • The instrument is not too low or too high to facilitate reading on a bench mark.
    • The length of the backsight should preferably not more than about 100m.
    • Preferably the fore and back sight distances should be equal and the foresight should be so located that it advances the line of levels.The process of setting up includes fixing the instrument and approximate levelling by leg adjustments.

  2. Levelling Up:

    3. The purpose of levelling is to make the vertical axis of the instrument truly vertical.

Principles of Levelling :

  1. Simple Levelling:

    2. Simple Levelling is performed,when the difference in elevation between two points is to be determined and both the points are visible from a single position of the level.

    • Let A and B be the two points,whose level difference has to be determined and let the level be setup at 'L'.A is a bench mark of R.L 100m.
    • The instrument is set up and levelled at L.The telescope is directed towards A and the staff reading is taken on a vertically held staff at a point A of known elevation. Thus backsight at A is 0.435.
    • The staff is then held vertically at point B whose elevation is to be found.The telescope is directed towards the staff and focussed.The foresight on B is 3.125m
    • The R.L of B is calculated,as follows.
      • Height of Instrument
      H.I = R.L of A + B.S.
      H.I = 100.00+0.435.
      =100.435.

  2. Differential Levelling :

    3. It is the method of levelling which requires more than one setting of the instrument. This method is performed under the following situations,

    • Two points who difference of elevation is required are situated large distance apart.
    • The difference in elevation between the two points is large.
    • Due to interverting of obstacles between the points. The differential levelling is also required in profile levelling,cross-sectioning,contouring and various other levelling operations.In fact,the levelling means the differential levelling,because in practice,a number of settings of the instrument are generally required.It is also called compound or continuous levelling.

Field Procedure in Levelling :

The Field work in levelling consists of reconnaissance,taking observations,booking in the field book and reduction of levels.

Booking and Reducing the levels :

There are two methods of booking and reducing the levels of point from the observed staff readings.

  1. Height of Instrument or Collimation method.
  2. Rise and fall method.
  1. Height of Instrument Method(Collimation Method):
    • In this method,the elevation of the plane of collimation(i.e.Height of Instrument) is found out by adding the backsight reading to the RL of the BM,on which the backsight reading to the RL of the BM,on which the backsight is taken.
    • The RL of intermediate points and the first change point is obtained by subtracting the respective staff readings from the Height of Instrument(HI).
    • Shift the instrument to the second position and again calculate the elevation of the plane of collimation,by adding the BS reading to the R.L of change point.
    • By means of backsight and foresight taken on the change point,Correlate the levels of the two planes of collimation.
    • Find out the reduced level of succesive points and the second change point by subtracting their staff readings from the elevation of the new plane of collimation.
    Arithmetical Check:
    ΣB.S-ΣF.S=Last R.L-First R.L.
  2. Rise and Fall method:
    • In this method,the difference of level between the consecutive points is determined by comparing their staff readings.
    • Obtain the rise and fall by calculating the difference between the consecutive staff readings.
    • If the forward staff reading(say F.S) is similar thean the immediately precedings staff readings(B.S or I.S),a rise is said to be occured.
    • Reduced level of each point is calculated by adding the rise or subtracting the fall from the reduced level of the preceding point.

Curvature and Refraction:

The effect of curvature and refraction should be taken into account while performing precise levelling and also if the sights are too long.(i.e.,more than 100m).

  1. Curvature :

    2. The surface of the earth is a curved and not straight.Hence,staff readings will be affected due to the curvature of the earth.Due to curvature of earth,we get larger staff readings than the true readings and hence the points sighted will appear to be lower than their true positions.

    In case of a long sight the horizontal line of sight will not coincide with the level line, due to the curvature of the earth.The vertical distance between the horizontal line of sight and the level line represents the effect of curvature of the earth.

    The curvature correction = Cc = BD
    Now,   OD2 = OA2+AD2
    or    (R+Cc)2 = R2+D2  where d=AD
    or R2+2R×Cc = R2+d2
    or     Cc(2R+Cc) = d2
    Cc  = d2/(2R+Cc)
    Since Cc is very small as compared to the radius of the earth R,we may say Cc = d2/2R Taking the radius of the earth as 6370 km. Cc=0.0785 d2 m Where     d = distance from instrument to staff in kilometres

    As the curvature increases the staff reading,the correction is therefore substractive.

    ∴ True staff reading =
    observed staff reading-0.0785 d2

  • Refraction :

    • The effect of refraction is to make the objects appear higher than their true positions. This happened due to the rays of light pass through layers of air of different densities and refract or bend down.As the ray from a staff flows a curved path,let us say AE.DE refraction correction and varies considerably with the climatic conditions.The average refraction correction can,however ,be taken as 1/7th of the curvature correction.

    Refraction correction

    Cr = (0.0785d2)/7

    =  0.0112 d2

    The correction due to refraction is additive.

  • Combined Correction :

    • Since,the effect of curvature and refraction when combined is to make the objects is to make the objects sighted will appear low,hence the overall correction is substractive.

    Combined correction = C = 0.0785 d2-0.0112d2
    =0.0673 d2
    ∴ True staff reading =
    observed staff reading-0.0673 d2

    Recriprocal levelling:

    To find the accurate level difference between two points, the level is placed exactly midway between two points and staff readings are taken, to satisfy the princple of equalising back sighy and foresight distance, i.e., to eliminate the errors due to inclined line of collimation, curvature and refraction. However in some cases (Eg : river or valley), it is not possible to setup the level midway between two points, and errors cannot be eliminated by ordinary levelling. In such cases, the method of reciprocal levlling is adopted.

    In reciprocal levelling, the level is setup near to each staff station point on the both banks of the river or valley and two sets pf staff readings are taken by holding the staff on both the points. The errors are completely eliminated and the true difference of level is equal to the mean of the two apparent difference of level.

    • Let A and B are two points located on the opposite banks of a river and there is a rise from A to B.
    • Let a1 and b1 be the staff readings on A and B with the instrument near A (fig. (a)), a2 and b2 be the readings on A and B with the instrument near B(fig(b)).
    • The combined error in the staff readings at the farther point due to curvature, refraction and the collimation line not being horizontal to "e". This will be the same for both the setups since the distance involved is the same.

    By observing fig (a) and (b) we can state that the correct readings on the father point = observed reading - curvature correction+refraction correction ± correction for collimation. In the present case, the sign of the collimation correction is negative according to the fig. Since the collimation line is shown to be inclined upwards.

    Let h = True difference of level between A & B.
       From fig(a)
    cross staff reading at A = a1, as the level is near A
    cross staff reading at B = b1 -e
    and h= a1- (b1 - e), as there is a rise from A to B -------(1) from fig (a)
    cross staff reading at B =b2, as the level is near B cross staff reading at A = a2 - e
    and h = (a2 - e ) - b2 -------(2)
    Adding eq (1) and (2) we get
       2h = a1 - (b1 - e) + (a2 -e) - b2
         = a1 - b1 + e + a2 - e - b2
        h = (a1 - b1 )+ (a2 - b2)/2 -------(3)
    Equating equations (1) and (2), we get
    a1 - (b1 - e) = (a2 - e) - b2
    a1 - b1 + e = a2 - e - b2
        2e = (a2 - b2) - (a1 - b1)
    nbsp   e = (a2 - b2) - (a1 - b1)/2

    Thus, by taking the mean difference of level obtained from both the stations, we get the correct value free not only from instrument error due to imperfect collimation, but also from the combined effect of curvature and refraction.
